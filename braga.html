Braga: The archbishops city or Portuguese Rome

Cultural monuments

Braga is one of the oldest Portuguese cities as well as one of the oldest shristian cities of the world. It was founded during the roman empire over 2000 years ago and named “Bracara Augusta”, since then the residents are called “Bracarence(s)”. In XI century the city was reorganized, receiving the designation of “Braga” by the bishop D. Pedro. In 1112, the city is donated by the king to the archbishops acquiring a great importance at Iberian level.

“És de Braga / Are you from Braga?” is a typical Portuguese expression said when someone leaves the door open… but why? At the beginning of XVI century archbishop D. Diogo de Sousa decided to open a new door on the city walls. Since there were no wars at the time and the city already extended for the outside of the walls, they never added a wooden door. The seven other doors were closed at night but that door was always open.

This expression is also associated with bracarences' great community spirit, since they all new each other and shared everything the house doors were never closed, welcoming everyone.

Braga has 82 churchs, 76 chappels, 2 sanctuaries and 1 catedral. Sé de Braga was founded in 1070 and is the oldest cathedral in Portugal. In this place are buried the father and mother of the first portuguese king, D. Afonso Henriques.

However the main attraction is the sanctuary of Bom Jesus do Monte where you can see the whole city. At this place there is a staircase with 573 steps but also one of seven elevators in the world moved by water weight, still operating daily.

Folclore music and Dance

The northern part of Portugal has a tradicional folclore dance with slight diferences in the dances styles and clothing.

Cavaquinho is a tradicional portuguese instrument, similar to hawain hukelele used for the instrumental, conjointly with guitars and reco-reco, a typical instrument made of wood whose sound results from the up and down movement with a wodden tool

Gastronomy

Since Braga is the oldest archdiocese of Portugal there is an affluence of conventual sweets, full of eggs and sugar, such as:

  • Tibia - tibia bone shaped sweet slightly crispy and filled with a sweet cream
  • Viuvas - “widows” were created in Convento dos remedios (convent of remedies) as a custard tart filled with egg yolds, sugar and a slight tast of nuts.
  • Pudim Abade de Priscos - A delicious pudding created by an abbot.
  • Fidalguinhos - “Little gentlemen” are small crispy biscuits with cinnamon taste, shaped like the crossed legs of gentlemen. Typically eaten during Easter.
  • Moletinhos - Similar to milk bread, these light and airy sweats are covered in a sugar syrup. Usually eaten during Saint Vicent' day and fathers' day.
